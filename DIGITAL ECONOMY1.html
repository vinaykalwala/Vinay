DIGITAL ECONOMY

 

What is the digital economy?

 

Back in the 1990s, when the internet was still a supplement to analog goods and services, the phrase "digital economy" first appeared. The main concern at that time was how developing digital channels may affect consumers and companies in the larger economy. The term "digital economy" today simply refers to any economic activity that takes place online. The capacity to gather, utilize, and analyze enormous volumes of machine-readable data in order to provide more individualized and meaningful experiences has actually led to the emergence of a data-driven economy. Companies are now able to innovate new business models and economic value in ways that were unthinkable thirty years ago thanks to the digital economy. In fact, at least two-thirds of executives claim that digital business activities significantly aid in the accomplishment of their main corporate goals.

 

The importance of the digital economy

 

The digital economy is more sophisticated than merely using technology to carry out already-existing functions on computers, despite the fact that some businesses and individuals do so. It goes beyond merely utilizing a computer to complete activities that were previously completed manually or with analog equipment.

 

Instead, the digital economy emphasizes the chance and requirement for businesses and people to utilize technology to carry out those duties more effectively, quicker, and more often in a different way than previously.

 

Additionally, the phrase refers to the capacity to use technology to carry out operations and take part in activities that weren't previously feasible. The associated idea of "digital transformation" includes such chances for current companies to do things more, differently, and in new ways.

 

Characteristics of the Digital Economy

 

How individuals and companies connect has changed significantly over the last ten years. Businesses have created their own business networks to link suppliers, consumers, and internal systems, building on the popularity of social networks. As a result, according to SAP Business Trends, global commerce will increase and reach $65 trillion by 2020.  With the growth of the Internet of Things, which is predicted to link 45 billion devices by 2020, the corporate environment is now filled with limitless opportunity. One must first comprehend the five key characteristics of the digital economy in order to properly adapt:

 

Tracked and digitalized.  For improved decision-making in a digital economy, analogue things provide digital signals that may be monitored, recorded, and analysed. Although the oil and gas sector has been at the forefront of digitization for more than 30 years, digitalized assets have only been used for expensive gear and equipment.  However, as sensor technology costs fall, operators are now able to move more processing into the field. For instance, businesses may link together several oilfields to boost prediction accuracy and profitability on a per-well basis.

 

Shared. The sharing economy drives the digital economy. Companies will soon simply purchase what is necessary and pay as they go. Buying only what is required reduces inventory costs, while buying utilisation as a service enables businesses to pay only for the services actually rendered and the value obtained.  Think about the potential for genuine cooperation between service station owners, third-party transporters, refineries, and consumer terminals. Tank refilling might be automated, best-buy scenarios could be used, and optimised truck routes for service station supplies could be planned by oil and gas corporations.

 

Personalized. Personalization of the consumer experience is another feature of the digital economy. Customers who choose certain brands receive customised goods and experiences from those businesses whenever and wherever they desire. Imagine supplying the preferred gasoline type to a petrol station atthe precise moment it expires. Alternatively, you may provide fleet drivers personalised routes that are tailored to their driving styles and preferences.

 

Connected. By connecting resources, vendors, employees, and stakeholders via wireless communications, individuals are able to make data-driven choices that enhance safety, efficiency, and visibility throughout the organisation. Oil production may be increased by interconnecting distant pipelines and utilising predictive maintenance.

 

Direct.  Additionally, the digital economy enables oil and gas firms to cut out the middleman, do away with pointless middlemen, and establish a more direct connection between buyer and seller. An ecosystem that has been simplified has less friction and reduces the entrance barrier for participants in other parts of the value chain. A excellent illustration of more direct activities is remote service monitoring.  Utilising remote intelligence eliminates the need for full-time, local staff by tracking, monitoring, managing, reporting, and resolving asset concerns throughout the service lifecycle.

 

Advantages of the digital economy

 

Encourages Internet Use: As a result, spending on everything associated has skyrocketed, including hardware, technical research, software, services, digital communication, etc. This ensures that the Internet and web-based businesses will continue to exist for the foreseeable future.

 

Growth of E-Commerce: Companies that embraced and adjusted to online commerce during the past 10 years have profited. The digital economy has caused the e-commerce sector to go into overdrive. Beyond only direct selling, purchasing, distribution, marketing, production, and selling have all advanced.

 

Additional Information: Because of the internet, consumers now have access to more information and alternatives. For example, it makes pricing comparisons between firms easier. It also puts knowledge at one's fingertips. This is especially important for vacationers. Finding accommodation prices and transport timetables could not have been feasible before the internet economy.

 

Saves Time: Previously, you had to trek into town to acquire office supplies. You may now place an order online and have it delivered the next day. Businesses benefit from lower labour expenses.

 

Reduced Costs: Businesses may save money on facility rentals by conducting the majority of their operations online. The capacity to send customised items straight from a manufacturer or warehouse.

 

Online Products and Services: The days of music CDs and movie DVDs are long gone. These items are now available to us digitally. Physical items are no longer in high demand. This also applies to financial services and insurance. There is no need to visit the bank if you can conduct all of your transactions online. Some products and services have been totally digitalized in this digital economy.

 

Discretion: In the digital economy, the great majority of transactions and payments take place online. Cash transactions are becoming increasingly rare. This improves economic openness while also reducing illegal money and market corruption. During the demonetization process, the government really advocated for online transactions in order to boost the digital economy.

 

Proximity: Customers may address inquiries and issues with a manufacturer or service provider more rapidly when they use direct customer care channels.

Global visibility. Companies may access additional markets now that goods and services are available to consumers at all times and from any location.

 

The digital economy is altering long-established manufacturing industries: Agriculture has already begun to reap the benefits of technological advancements. Crops are linked to farmers via mobile applications, which provide real-time reports on quality, soil, and irrigation to help farmers make management decisions.

 

Reduced Entry Barriers: Certain aspects of the digital economy make it easier for new enterprises to enter certain industries. If a businessperson has an original concept that gets embraced, they might produce a new product that competes with established enterprises. The internet economy has enabled previously inconceivable services, ranging from dating apps to online grocery deliveries.

 

Produces critical data that can lead to new discoveries: Governments and non-profit organisations may benefit from the vast amount of data generated by knowing more about the status of the economy. Using a smartphone app to track the spread of COVID-19, for example, may reveal the locations of growing local hotspots.

 

The disadvantages of the digital economy

 

Despite the possibilities of new start-ups, monopoly-holding firms have grown to dominate many aspects of the digital economy. For example, Amazon has a monopoly on online sales, forcing many firms to use the Amazon marketplace to interact with clients who purchase there frequently. Google and Facebook have incredibly high brand loyalty and market domination in their respective areas. As a result, a few tech behemoths are now extremely lucrative. Because of its dominating position, Google can demand high prices for online advertising, but Amazon has the market strength to undercut other traditional bookshops.

 

Less Community: A physical bookshop may act as a community hub. It may offer events like book signings, and guests may enjoy the sensation of browsing actual books. When the digital alternative undercuts established enterprises, traditional bookshops go out of business.

 

Despite the fact that books are less expensive, the actual connection between vendors and buyers, which was a critical aspect of the purchase process, is no longer available.

 

The addictive aspects of technology: While obtaining bus timetables online is much simpler than on paper, this potential time savings may be outweighed by the time we waste watching Facebook, Twitter, and internet searches. Furthermore, the sheer volume of information may overwhelm us and cause us to lose sight of what is important.

 

Because of social media, more graphic content has appeared.

 

Because of the anonymity and distance afforded by social media, it has become increasingly common for people to launch personal attacks, spread theories, and exchange pornographic or violent content. Because of the digital economy, negative content for human well-being is growing.

 

Problems with Privacy: Data collecting and utilisation are becoming very profitable companies. Facebook collects a wide range of user data, which political interests have acquired in order to target individuals with highly relevant political advertisements.

 

Employment Loss: As our dependence on technology grows, so does our reliance on human resources. The growth of the digital economy may result in considerable job losses. As operations become more automated, the requirement for human resources decreases. Consider internet banking as one example.

 

In the digital economy, complex technologies and procedures are required. Platforms must be developed and maintained by specialised and trained individuals. These are few, particularly in rural and semi-rural areas.

 

Significant investment: The digital economy requires a strong infrastructure, an efficient Internet, and strong mobile networks. This entire procedure requires a significant amount of time and money. In a developing country like ours, infrastructure and network development is a long, time-consuming, and costly process.
