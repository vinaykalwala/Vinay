CYBER BULLYING AND IT’S TYPES AND IT’S PREVENTIONS

What Is Cyberbullying?

Sharing or transmitting inaccurate, damaging, or offensive material about someone online is considered cyberbullying. It can cross the line into being illegal if it involves publicly humiliating someone else by disclosing their personal details.1 The purpose of cyberbullying is to cause emotional, psychological, and occasionally even bodily harm. It is spiteful and menacing in character.

A prevalent misperception regarding cyberbullying is that it is simple to avoid or ignore; nevertheless, this is frequently not the case. People may use the internet whenever they want, thus bullying can continue for a long time.1 Additionally, although cyberbullying is most frequently associated with middle school and high school, it may also happen to adults and can seriously harm a person's reputation.

Online networking offers advantages and disadvantages.

Although the Internet and social media platforms have improved our lifestyles and changed how we interact, they may also expose us to new dangers. One instance that can have catastrophic repercussions on young people is cyberbullying. When someone is threatened, abused, or degraded online, such as through social media posts or SMS messages, this is known as cyberbullying. Cyberbullying has recently attracted increasing attention and reputation due to its use of technology. It is a crime that does not just occur in schools or on playgrounds.

What promotes online bullying? It doesn't need physical power or a face-to-face confrontation, unlike conventional bullying. Cyberbullies may be anyone with access to the Internet and a digital device, such as a smartphone, computer, or tablet. Whether through social networking sites or instant messaging, there are no set hours during which it must occur.

48 percent of parents feel their children are more likely to be bullied online than on a playground, according to the 2016 Norton Cyber Security Insights Report. Parents and school administrators should prioritise finding ways to stop cyberbullying. But preventing bullying is a challenging task.

Because many sites don't try to confirm users' genuine identities, a cyberbully can use an alias and maintain their anonymity. This veil of anonymity encourages cyberbullying and frequently serves to magnify the severity of a cyberbully's nasty and hurtful behaviour. Anyone, including small children, middle schoolers, and high school students, might be the target.

Types of Cyberbullying

Catfishing

A cyberbully uses catfishing to prey on their victim's feelings. When a victim is being cyberbullied, the aggressor develops a false online identity and poses as someone else. The bully can then interact with a victim while posing as them, starting an online relationship. The victim can eventually come to trust the internet user and divulge private information to them. The cyberbully might then utilise this information to expose or humiliate the victim by making them look bad.

Flaming

Flaming is the act of publicly criticising a victim or sending them offensive messages. A victim of cyberbullying is flamed in an effort to provoke them into an online altercation.

Denigration

By transmitting, uploading, or publishing false material about a victim online, a cyberbully denigrates the person. Typically, denigration takes the form of malicious rumours and gossip about a victim.

Impersonation

By making posts under the victim's name on social media and in chat rooms, a cyberbully can impersonate a victim. By doing this, a victim runs the risk of receiving retribution from others as a result of the bully's online comments.

Trickery

A cyberbully may get friendly with a victim to the point that the victim feels at ease disclosing critical information. The information the victim reveals is subsequently made public by the bully in an effort to degrade, defame, or otherwise hurt the victim.

Bogus profiles

Cyberbullies are able to create phoney internet personas on behalf of their targets. Without the victims' permission, they can publish fraudulent content using these accounts in the identities of their victims.

Prevention

Cyberbullying prevention is frequently overlooked. If not completely eliminated, cyberbullying would be addressed or stopped with the adoption of an effective preventative strategy.

Three preventative strategies that can be taken into account are as follows:

Follow your child on social media:

To some, it may seem intrusive to your child's online privacy, but you should follow your child on social media at the very least if you are a parent, guardian, cousin, close family, or other responsible adult.

Installing a monitoring programme:

This would be regarded as a severe invasion of privacy, yet the effects are lovely. This might be applied to a situation involving a young child and would also stop a youngster from internet adult material. Parents and guardians can go at the interaction records every month or every three months.

Awareness:

Children should be made aware of the possibility of cyberbullying as a result of sharing a selfie photo or video that may not be well received by others. Children should be given three directions. First, a definition of cyberbullying, guidelines for appropriate chat/talk time, and examples would allow the youngster to understand it better. What to share and what not to share comes in second. The third is to reassure the youngster that you would assist them in resolving the situation if, by chance, they become a victim of cyberbullying on any social media site.

The relationship between stopping cyberbullying and violating children's privacy is inverse. In other words, if we strive to boost prevention, privacy will be thrown out the window, and if we try to it will be challenging to stop bullying if you allow them unlimited privacy.
