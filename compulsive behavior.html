Obsessive Behavior

 

An obsessive person will get up and check the latch 10 or 15 times, continually concerned that the door is unlocked. This behavior becomes habitual and is characterized by an overpowering desire to fulfill them.

 

Compulsive behavior occurs when a person continually engages in a behavior that does not necessarily bring relief or a reward but rather repeats the behavior in an attempt to eliminate particular obsessions. People who indulge in obsessive behavior frequently feel stuck by them and find it extremely difficult to stop.

 

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a mental health problem characterized by obsessive thoughts and compulsive behaviors. A person must have one or both of these to be diagnosed with the illness. Furthermore, the symptoms must cause anguish, and severe impairment, and consume a large amount of a person's time.

 

Obsessive thoughts are constant, unwelcome, and distressing. They are difficult to prevent or stop, and they create a great deal of distress, anxiety, or fear. Obsessive thoughts can be about anything, from a dread of being contaminated with germs to recurring violent visions or a persistent worry that work has been finished. These thoughts, whatever their substance, are unreasonable and practically difficult to control.

 

Compulsive behaviors are acts taken to try to relieve the anguish caused by obsessive thoughts, to stop the thoughts, or to prevent something awful from happening. There is typically no logical relationship between the compulsions and the ideas, but they are often related, such as when someone is terrified of germs and compulsively washes his or her hands. A person with OCD, like obsessive thoughts, finds it difficult to regulate these behaviors, even when they are illogical, and feels forced to indulge in them.

 

What Is Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)?

 

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is characterized by persistent, unwelcome thoughts, ideas, or feelings (obsessions). They feel compelled to perform something repetitively (compulsions) to get rid of the ideas. Hand washing/cleaning, checking on things, and mental acts such as (counting) or other activities can all severely interfere with a person's daily activities and social interactions.

 

Many people who do not have OCD experience distressing thoughts or repetitive behavior. However, these are not usually disruptive to normal life. Thoughts are constant and intrusive in those with OCD, and behaviors are rigid. Not doing the behaviors frequently produces significant discomfort, which is frequently associated with a specific fear of severe repercussions (to oneself or loved ones) if the behaviors are not accomplished.

 

Obsessional thoughts and/or compulsions that are time-consuming (more than one hour per day), cause significant distress, and impede work or social functioning are required for an OCD diagnosis. OCD affects 2-3% of people in the United States, with women being somewhat more affected than males. OCD is most commonly diagnosed in childhood, adolescence, or early adulthood. Some persons may have OCD symptoms but do not meet the full diagnostic criteria.

 

Obsessions:

 

Obsessions are recurring and persistent thoughts, impulses, or visions that elicit negative emotions like worry, fear, or revulsion. Many persons with OCD recognize that these are mental constructs that are excessive or irrational. However, logic and reasoning cannot alleviate the distress caused by these unwanted ideas. Compulsions are commonly used by people with OCD to alleviate the distress caused by obsessional thinking or to undo perceived dangers. They may also try to ignore or conceal their obsessions, or they may divert their attention to other activities.

Compulsions:

 

Compulsions are repetitive behaviors or mental acts that a person feels compelled to execute as a result of an obsession. The behaviors usually temporarily avoid or decrease a person's distress caused by an obsession, and they are then more inclined to do the same in the future. Compulsions can be excessive responses to an obsession (for example, excessive hand washing owing to contamination anxiety) or activities that are wholly unconnected to the obsession. A continual repetition of routines may fill the day in the most severe cases, making a normal routine impossible.

5 Common Types of OCD

 

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a psychiatric illness in which persons have stressful and recurring thoughts (obsessions), which frequently lead to an action they feel compelled to execute (compulsions). They may need to do these compulsions once or several times before they feel more at peace. Obsessions and compulsions differ from person to person, however, doctors commonly classify them into categories. People frequently have symptoms from multiple categories, which might change over time. Here are a few examples of the most prevalent.

 

1. Organising

 

This is possibly the most well-known type of OCD, and it involves obsessions with objects being exactly in the proper spot or symmetrical. For example, someone may feel compelled to keep all wall hangings level, all labels on pantry cans facing outward, or everything on their workstation totally clean. If the person does not follow through on the compulsions to have things precisely so, they may experience worry or even fear that their lack of organization will cause them or they're loved one damage.

 

2. Weakness

 

Contamination OCD is based on two broad concepts. The first is the belief that non-viral illnesses can be transferred by touch or proximity. The second is that commonplace objects, thoughts, and words can "contaminate" a person, making them feel dirty. People of this type frequently feel the need to continuously wash their hands and clean their belongings in order to avoid spreading the perceived contamination. They may be afraid of making themselves or others ill by being careless, or they may feel disgusted and uncomfortable with "unclean" stuff, leading to avoidance of specific products, persons, or locations.

 

3. Pervasive Thoughts

 

Intrusive thoughts cause distressing and frequently repulsive concepts to enter into people's heads seemingly at random. These obsessions can include hurting a loved one, inflicting harm to a stranger, or even the belief that simply thinking about something increases the likelihood of it happening. To silence these obsessions, a person may need to perform an action, such as speaking something aloud or mentally repeating something.

 

People who have intrusive obsessions may have aggressive or dangerous thoughts, but they do not agree with them or act on them. In fact, these notions are so diametrically opposed to how people feel that they frequently become concerned that their minds even generated the concept in the first place.

 

4. Thoughts 

 

This type is similar to intrusive thought-based OCD, but there are some significant differences. The thoughts that become ingrained in the mind of someone suffering from rumination-based OCD are not unattractive or distressing. Instead, they could be philosophical, religious, or metaphysical conundrums (basically, unanswered questions).

 

People who have ruminations will be preoccupied with this topic for some time and may neglect their tasks as they strive to find an answer. Because these concerns frequently have no conclusive answers, people may feel unfulfilled or empty after spending so much time thinking about them.

 

5. Double-checking

 

Checking is an obsession in which a person is preoccupied with the possibility of causing damage or injury by being careless. Checking doors to make sure they're locked, stoves to make sure the burners are turned off, or their wallet to make sure their credit cards, IDs, and cash are all still there are examples of compulsions. They may need to double-check something or even look at it for a while before they feel more comfortable.

Treatment

 

Treatment for obsessive-compulsive disorder may not result in a cure, but it can help put symptoms under control so that they do not dominate your everyday life. Some patients may require long-term, continuous, or more intensive treatment depending on the severity of their OCD.

 

Psychotherapy and medicines are the two basic therapies for OCD. Treatment is often most effective when a combination of these is used.

 

Psychotherapy

 

Many patients with OCD benefit from cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), a type of psychotherapy. A component of CBT therapy called exposure and response prevention (ERP) includes gradually exposing you to a feared object or preoccupation, such as dirt, and teaching you how to resist the temptation to do your compulsive rituals. ERP requires effort and practice, but you may benefit from it.

 

Medications

 

Certain psychiatric drugs can aid in the control of OCD obsessions and compulsions. Antidepressants are frequently tried first.

 

The following antidepressants have been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of OCD:

What to think about when it comes to medications

 

Here are some points to talk with your doctor about concerning OCD medications:

 

Selecting a medicine:

 

In general, the goal is to successfully control symptoms while using the least amount of medication. It is fairly uncommon to test numerous medications before finding one that works effectively. To properly control your symptoms, your doctor may recommend more than one medicine. It can take weeks or months to observe an improvement in symptoms after starting a drug.

 

Adverse consequences:

All psychiatric drugs may cause negative effects. Consult your doctor about potential side effects and any health monitoring that may be required while taking mental medications. Also, notify your doctor if you notice any troubling side effects.

 

Suicide is a possibility:

 

Although most antidepressants are typically safe, the FDA requires that all antidepressants have black box warnings, the most stringent prescription cautions. Children, teenagers, and young adults under the age of 25 may have an increase in suicidal thoughts or behavior when taking antidepressants, particularly in the first few weeks or when the dose is altered. If you have suicidal thoughts, call your doctor or seek emergency care right away. Keep in mind that antidepressants, by increasing mood, are more likely to minimize suicide risk in the long run.

 

Other drug interactions:

 

Tell your doctor about any additional prescription or over-the-counter drugs, herbs, or vitamins you use when taking an antidepressant. When coupled with certain drugs or herbal supplements, some antidepressants can make other prescriptions less effective and create hazardous responses.

 

Antidepressants are being discontinued:

 

Antidepressants are not considered addictive, but physical dependence (which is not the same as addiction) can occur. As a result, abruptly discontinuing medication or skipping several doses can result in withdrawal-like symptoms known as discontinuation syndrome. Even if you're feeling better, don't stop taking your medication without consulting your doctor; you could experience a resurgence of OCD symptoms. Work with your doctor to reduce your dose gradually and carefully.
