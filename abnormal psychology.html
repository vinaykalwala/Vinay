Abnormal Psychology

 

Introduction:

 

The area of psychology known as "abnormal psychology" is concerned with examining odd patterns of thinking, emotion, and behaviour that may or may not be indicative of a mental condition. The majority of the time, this area of psychology deals with behaviour in a clinical setting, even if many behaviors might be seen as deviant. There has been a long history of efforts to comprehend and rein in behaviour that has been regarded abnormal or deviant (statistically, functionally, ethically, or in other ways), and the methods used have frequently varied among cultures. The definition of "abnormal" remains a key question in the subject of abnormal psychology, which seeks to explain a variety of situations using a variety of ideas drawn from both the wider field of psychology and other disciplines. Historically, there has been a distinction between psychological and biological reasons,reflecting a dualism in philosophy with reference to the mind-body issue.

 

What is abnormal psychology?

 

One of the main sub fields of psychology that focuses on behaviour, thinking, or psychopathology is abnormal psychology. Since "normal behaviour" has no universally accepted definition, many doctors disagree on what constitutes abnormal psychology. Instead, abnormal psychology-trained counselors and psychologists concentrate on a person's behaviors and ideas and the extent to which they impact their everyday lives.

 

Somatogenic and psychogenic hypotheses are two that mental health professionals feel are responsible for abnormality. Psychogenic theorists hold that aberrant behaviour is produced by psychological imbalances, whereas somatogenic theorists hold that abnormal behaviour is caused by diseases or bodily problems. They are often comparing which attitudes and behaviors stray from societal norms. When deciding what is aberrant inside their client, they take into account the traits of gender, culture, context and circumstance, historical text, and age. Psychologists will use a range of techniques to address aberrant behaviors or ideas. The parts that follow will go through these.

 

Types of Abnormal Psychology: 

 

It is crucial to first go over the many kinds of aberrant psychiatric problems. Ten disorders in this area are listed in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM), which was created by the American Psychological Association:

 

l  Anxiety conditions

l  diseases of compulsive behavior

l  Diseases caused by traumatic stress

l  Mood issues

l  psychological problems

l  Schizophrenia

l  Delusional conditions

l  diseases caused by drug usage

l  Disorders of dissociation

l  problems of impulse control

 

These diseases are very diverse from one another in terms of behaviors and ideas. As a result, a clinician treating them would need to adopt a different therapeutic stance.

 

Psychoanalytical Perspective:

 

The founder of psychoanalysis, Sigmund Freud, argued that unconscious memories, urges, and ideas were the source of abnormal psychiatric diseases. Clinicians that adopt this viewpoint concentrate on memories, actions, attitudes, and even dreams. Disconnections between memories, ideas, and behaviors are common in people with dissociative disorders. These are frequently resolved to utilize a psychoanalytic viewpoint.

 

Medical Perspectives:

 

When diagnosing an abnormal psychological illness, medical professionals emphasize biological elements. Genetic traits, hormone disorders, infections, and physical ailments are all taken into account when making a diagnosis and treatment plan, which frequently include medication. A mental illness called schizophrenia results in hallucinations and delusions. According to research, the disease is influenced by both heredity and brain chemistry (excess dopamine).

 

Psychological Perspective:

 

Behaviourists concentrate on a person's observable behaviors and emphasize positive behaviors while minimizing bad ones. Instead of focusing on the underlying problem, these physicians are more interested in the behaviour itself. Therefore, they would use techniques like classical and operant conditioning while creating a therapy plan. Breathing exercises would be used by someone with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) to help them feel less anxious.

 

Cognitive Perspective:

 

In abnormal psychology, the cognitive viewpoint focuses on one's own thoughts and beliefs. In order to influence the client's reactions, treatment frequently entails changing the client's ideas. As a result, the behavioral approach—also known as cognitive-behavioral therapy, or CBT—is frequently employed in combination with cognitive therapy. Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) patients engage with therapists to unravel the ideas behind their dread, which helps reduce many of the compulsive behaviors.

 

The social and cultural perspective:

 

According to the social and cultural perspective, aberrant behavior is acquired within a social environment, which might include the family, community, and culture. It is thought that cultural factors, learned through learning and cognitive processes, have a significant role in developing aberrant behavior. For instance, psychiatric illnesses like bulimia and anorexia nervosa are more prevalent in Western countries, which place a high priority on a slender female body.

 

The causes of abnormal behavior

 

Mental diseases and aberrant behavior are influenced by a variety of variables. To make it complete, an integrated multi-path model for the components that contribute to aberrant behavior is offered. The main elements are sociocultural, sociological, psychological, and biological. Because several factors combine to affect anomalous behavior, it is called integrative because no one element alone has an impact on behavior.

 

Biological Constraints

The least developed understood underlying causes of aberrant behavior are biological factors. Genes, the human brain, the nervous system, neurochemicals, hormones, neurotransmitters, neuroplasticity, etc. are a few examples of biological influences.

 

The knowledge that genes predispose a person to any susceptibility or sickness is aided by the fact that many mental illnesses or aberrant behavior have genetic etiologies. This brings up the topic of heredity, the hereditary transfer of features that may contribute to the emergence of deviant behavior. There may be a hereditary component underpinning several well-known diseases including schizophrenia, phobias, and depression.

 

Thoughts, emotions, motivation, memory, controlling one's hunger, controlling one's voluntary and involuntary actions, and many more particular activities are all controlled by the human brain. The brain and body have completed their whole communication.

 

Psychological factors

The formation of the personality (psychodynamic factors), the function of learning (behavioral elements), cognitions (cognitive factors), and the suppression of natural wants and aspirations (humanistic factors) are among the psychological causes driving aberrant behavior.

 

The psychodynamic viewpoint sees internal tensions, fixations, and early events as contributing to the emergence of disordered behaviour. This concept states that the id (pleasure principle), ego (reality principle), and superego (moral principle) make up personality. Conflict in these three areas causes emotional symptoms, which have the potential to reappear and drive inappropriate behaviour into the unconscious or start employing defence mechanisms.

 

According to the behavioural perspective, anomalous behaviour is the outcome of observation, modelling, learning, and reinforcement. This viewpoint explains the fundamental causes of anomalous behaviour using studies like Pavlov's classical conditioning, Skinner's rat operant conditioning, and Bandura's observational learning. For instance, a youngster can learn about their family member's fear of dogs by watching them.

 

Social Factors

The development and control of aberrant behavior is influenced by a variety of elements, including family, parenting methods, peer interactions, social support, a sense of belonging, employment, and many more. These underpinning social elements influence how a person responds to a certain stressor or life experience. Poorly constructed, meaningless, or nonexistent social ties might make people more vulnerable to dysfunctional coping mechanisms and the emergence of deviant behavior.

 

Children raised inappropriate parenting practices have a lower risk of mental illnesses, whereas children raised in harmful parenting practices, such as uninvolved parents, lack connection and a sense of belonging, and do poorly in school, among other things.

 

Other social issues, such as unhealthy peer interactions, bullying, an unbalanced family, a troubled marriage, poor communication, and imbalanced relationships, might contribute to aberrant behavior.

 

Generally included are the following characteristics:

 

Rarely can abnormal behavior occur: However, a definition based solely on statistics is insufficient. A few good, desirable, and healthy behaviors are also sporadic. The way a person behaves or works is unaffected by other unusual traits or behaviors. Therefore, something shouldn't be classified as abnormal merely because it is odd or uncommon.

 

Unusual behavior upsets others: These actions may disturb the person, or they may upset and annoy other people.

 

A person's capacity to operate is impacted by abnormal behavior: People who exhibit these behaviors may find it difficult to go about their everyday lives regularly, which can have an impact on their relationships, jobs, studies, and personal lives.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Slowly but surely, abnormal behavior takes hold, eroding a person's mental stability. When improper behaviour is first ignored and misdiagnosed, it gradually transforms into various psychiatric diseases. Understanding the causes of abnormal conduct as well as the idea of abnormal behavior and how it varies from culture or society is the first step in treating or controlling any aberrant activity. The individual may be handled appropriately after the underlying causes have been revealed. Now, after a little amount of time without any therapy, a person can heal on their own. Others could require appropriate care, depending on their circumstances or mental state.
