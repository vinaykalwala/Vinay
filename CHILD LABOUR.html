What is child labour?

 

Child labour is a worldwide problem that stops youngsters from reaching their full potential. Child labour is a breach of children's rights since it can cause mental or bodily injury, expose them to dangerous conditions, or prevent them from attending school.

 

Not every job performed by children is considered child labour. When a youngster works to improve their life skills without jeopardising their health, well-being, or schooling, there is no problem. Often, older children will do chores or little jobs for their parents in order to earn pocket money or obtain work experience. This work prepares kids for adult employment.

 

However, millions of children labour in child labour around the world. They work in fields, factories, mines, as servants or maids, or as street vendors or market vendors. Girls are more likely than boys to perform household tasks such as cleaning, cooking, and serving.

 

Work that minors may be forced to do may also be unlawful, such as drug gangs or prostitution. Many youngsters may not get paid for their efforts, but they will be fed and housed. Most youngsters will not be compensated for missed work if they fall unwell or injured.

 

All of these forms of child employment can keep a child from attending school and hinder their ability to reach their full potential. Many child employees do not attend school or drop out.

 

Education is a tried-and-true technique for minimising child labour. Lack of access to education perpetuates the cycle of exploitation, illiteracy, and poverty, restricting children's future prospects and compelling them to take low-wage labour as adults and raise their own children in hardship. Children who have access to education have the potential to break the cycle of poverty that is at the root of child labour.

 

Child labour is frequently defined as employment that deprives children of their youth, potential, and dignity, and is destructive to their physical and mental development. It refers to work that is mentally, physically, socially, or morally dangerous and harmful to children; and/or interferes with their schooling by: denying them the opportunity to attend school; forcing them to leave school early; or requiring them to combine school attendance with excessively long and heavy work.

 

The definition of "child labour" relies on the child's age, the type and hours of work performed, the conditions under which it is performed, and the goals pursued by specific countries. The answer differs depending on the country.

 

Child Labour Laws and Enforcement

 

The Employment Act of 1980 establishes a minimum age of 15 years for non-hazardous industrial work, while youngsters as young as 13 can work in the commercial sector.3436 It is prohibited to employ children under the age of 18 in mines, quarries, or underground work, or in any other industry that poses a risk to their safety or health.3437 The law allows children under the age of 15 to work in family industrial firms or technical schools under supervision, but only for six hours per day and 33 hours per week.3438 The Ministry of Labour is in charge of enforcing child labour rules, although its effectiveness is restricted by a staff shortage.3439

 

Children all across the world are routinely engaged in non-harmful kinds of paid and unpaid labour. They are regarded as child labourers, however, when they are either too young to work or are engaging in dangerous activities that may jeopardise their physical, mental, social, or educational development. In the least developed countries, slightly more than one in every four children (aged 5 to 17) is involved in labour that is harmful to their health and development.

 

The International Labour Organisation (ILO) Convention No. 138 concerning the minimum age for admission to employment and Recommendation No. 146 (1973); the International Labour Organisation (ILO) Convention No. 182 concerning the prohibition and immediate action for the elimination of the worst forms of child labour and Recommendation No. 190 (1999); and the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child. These treaties define child labour and serve as the foundation for child labour legislation established by signatory countries.

 

5 Facts about Child Labour:

 

1.THE AGRICULTURE SECTOR HAS THE HIGHEST PREVALENCE OF CHILD LABOUR:

 

According to ILO figures, 70% of child labour occurs in agriculture. Much of the work done by children in agriculture is inappropriate for their age, dangerous, or interferes with their education. A child under the legal working age who is recruited to herd cattle, a child who applies pesticides, and a child who works all night on a fishing boat and is too weary to attend to school the next day are all examples of child labour.

 

2.NOT ALL CHILD WORK IS LABOUR:

 

Participation of children in work that does not jeopardise their health and personal development or interfere with their schooling is generally regarded as positive; for example, helping their parents around the house, assisting in a family business, or earning pocket money outside of school hours and during school holidays.

 

"Child labour" is frequently defined as employment that deprives children of their youth, potential, and dignity, is damaging to their physical and mental development, and interferes with their education, and thus must be eliminated.

 

3.CHILD LABOUR EMPLOYS 152 MILLION CHILDREN:

 

According to the International employment Organisation (ILO), around 152 million children aged 5 to 17 are involved in child employment. They are found in harmful situations or environments, such as mining, dealing with chemicals and pesticides in agriculture, or operating dangerous machinery. They serve as domestic labour in houses, factories, and plantations. Boys and girls are equally likely to be involved in child labour in almost all regions, with girls significantly more likely to be involved in household work.

 

4.CHILD LABOUR CONTINUES THE POVERTY CYCLE:

 

There is a clear correlation between household poverty and child labour, and child labour perpetuates poverty through generations by keeping impoverished children out of school and limiting their opportunities for upward social mobility. Human capital depletion has been related to slower economic growth and social development.

 

5.CHILD LABOUR IS DECREASING:

 

Child labour is avoidable, and it is within our grasp to eradicate. Since 2000, the number of children participating in child labour has decreased by one-third. Global March works with governments, civil society, communities, and businesses to advocate against child labour by supporting action to strengthen education and child protection systems in order to improve access to quality education and eliminate child labour, as well as the creation and implementation of strong laws - from the ground up.

 

What is hazardous child labour or hazardous work?

 

Hazardous child employment is defined as work in hazardous or unhealthy conditions that could result in a child being killed, hurt, or made ill as a result of low safety and health standards and working conditions. It can cause permanent disability, illness, and psychological harm. Often, health problems induced by child work do not manifest themselves until the child reaches adulthood.

 

With an estimated 79 million children aged 5-17 working in dangerous conditions in a variety of sectors such as agriculture, mining, construction, manufacturing, as well as hotels, bars, restaurants, markets, and domestic service, hazardous child labour is the largest category of the worst forms of child labour. It can be found in both industrialised and developing countries.

 

Children are more exposed to occupational dangers than adults because their bodies and minds are still developing, and the repercussions of hazardous work are typically more devastating and long-lasting for them.

 

When discussing child employment, it is critical to expand the ideas of work hazard and risk1 as they apply to adult employees to incorporate the developmental characteristics of children. Because children are still growing, they have unique traits and needs, and when considering occupational hazards and risks, their impact on children's physical, cognitive (thought/learning), behavioural, and emotional development must be considered.

 

How to Prevent and Eliminate Child Labour

 

The elimination of child trafficking, poverty, free and compulsory education, and basic living conditions can significantly minimize the problem. The World Bank and the International Monetary Fund can aid in the abolition of poverty by lending to developing countries.

 

Strict enforcement of employment regulations is also required to avoid exploitation by political parties or multinational corporations. Many revisions to the current child labour prohibition statute are required in order to apply strong measures to control the problem. The minimum age of fourteen years must be raised to at least eighteen years. More vocations should be added to the list of hazardous activities that are prohibited by law.

Encouraging the Right to Education

 

Every youngster in India is entitled to an education. Our constitution clearly states that every child under 14 is entitled to free education. Education is the most effective means of combating child labor; unfortunately, many low-income families do not allow their children to attend school. They believe that earning is essential for the family's survival. The government and non-governmental organizations like ours encourage parents to send their children to school. In order to extend the right to education, motivational and encouraging videos are created.
