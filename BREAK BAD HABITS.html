BREAK BAD HABITS

Introduction:

 

Bad habits disrupt your life and keep you from achieving your goals. They endanger both your emotional and physical well being. They also squander your time and efforts.

 

So why do we continue to do them? Is there anything you can do about it, most importantly?

 

I've previously talked on the science of habit formation, so let's now focus on the practice of making real-world adjustments. How do you get rid of your negative habits and replace them with positive ones?

 

I don't have all of the solutions, but keep reading and I'll share what I've learned about breaking harmful habits.

 

Breaking Bad Habits

 

We all have bad habits that we wish to break - ways of thinking and doing that give us grief and issues and can harm us and others. It might feel almost difficult to break free at times.

 

Buddha taught us about the reasons of our unpleasant behaviors as well as how to let go. We may gain the abilities to detect our unpleasant behaviors and learn to adopt strategies to reach inner peace and liberation via personal observation and training.

 

This course continues our exploration of the classic work How to Understand the Mind. These seminars are appropriate for everyone, including new mediators.

 

What factors contribute to harmful habits?

 

The majority of your poor behaviors are triggered by two factors.

 

Boredom and stress

 

Most negative habits are merely a means of coping with stress and boredom. Everything from chewing your nails to going on a shopping spree to binge drinking every weekend to squandering time on the internet may be an easy way to cope with stress and boredom.

 

But this does not have to be the case. You can educate yourself on new and healthy methods to deal with stress and boredom, which you can then replace your old behaviors with.

 

Of course, sometimes the tension or boredom that is visible is driven by deeper concerns.

 

How to Break Bad Habits:

 

You probably have a nasty habit that you'd like to break. Overeating, excessive screen time, little sleep, saying "yes" too frequently, missing deadlines, procrastinating, breaking commitments, or succumbing to rage are all examples.

 

These behaviour may not worry you at first. But there comes a time when you say, "enough is enough." Perhaps your habit is affecting your coworkers or family. Or perhaps you've suddenly realized that your habit is keeping you from accomplishing your professional objectives. Whatever the cause, there is no such thing as a terrible moment to end a harmful habit.

 

The nine tactics listed below can help you get started, but keep in mind that there is no "one-size-fits-all" solution:

 

Determine the Sources of Your Habit

 

Examine your habit carefully. Ask yourself when you generally conduct the behaviour, who you're with, what happens, how you feel, and the "payoff" you receive when you do it.

 

Bad habits often form because we believe they will help our lives in some way. Some people, for example, smoke or overeat in order to relieve stress. Other habits emerge as a result of societal or cultural expectations. The practice of refusing praises, for example, is widespread in Western countries since it is regarded as the "polite" thing to do.

 

Understanding why a habit arose can assist you in dealing with it in the most effective manner.

 

Develop Your Self-Awareness

 

Consider what you need to do or how you might adjust your surroundings to offer yourself the best chance of successfully quitting your habit.

 

Gretchen Rubin argues in her book "Better Than Before" that in order to do so, we must first identify our "dominant tendency" - the way we respond to stresses such as deadlines, demands, and decision making.

 

Each of us, according to Rubin, is either an Upholder, a Questioner, an Obliger, or a Rebel. When it comes to quitting a bad habit, each inclination has various requirements.

 

Upholders must make a deliberate decision to change. Questioners want to know how their habit affects them. Obligers require accountability from someone. And rebels must effect change on their own terms.

 

Make use of obstacles and substitutes

 

Positive psychologist Shawn Anchor argues in his 2010 book, "The Happiness Advantage," that you may change undesirable habits by placing hurdles in place that prevent you from automatically carrying out the behaviour. For example, if you find yourself frequently checking Twitter, you may use an app to restrict your access.

 

You might also try to avoid the people, places, and events that set off your undesirable behaviour. For example, if you wish to quit gossiping at work. You know you gossip the most during lunch with certain coworkers, so you decide to eat at a different time and with different individuals.

 

You can also search for positive behaviors to replace undesirable behaviors. The replacement must be available for this to operate.

 

Look for a Role Model

 

Look for someone who has broken a habit similar to yours and received the benefits. This demonstrates that it is both feasible and worthwhile to break your own.

 

Choose someone you admire and like. If it's someone you know, inquire as to how they accomplished it. You might even request that they advise or mentor you while you work through your habit. It might also be a celebrity or a business leader. whether so, see whether they've published a book or look for interviews online.

 

Treat Yourself

 

As you begin to break your habit, reward yourself with a small treat every now and then to make you feel good about your success.

 

When rewards follow immediately after positive behaviour, they are most effective. Choose something you actually desire and will appreciate. Your brain may begin to correlate your new, positive behaviour with the reward over time, reinforcing your new "good" habit.

 

Pause for thought before reacting

Meditation practice helps you improve the ability to swiftly notice when your mind wanders. Slowing down the frantic churning in your brain allows you to better grasp your ideas and feelings.

 

After a few months of practice, this ability will manifest itself in your daily life as more space between an emotional trigger and your reaction. This liberates you from your conditioned reflexes, allowing you to interact with the problems of the current moment more intelligently and effectively.

 

So, if your automatic reaction to stress is to put a cigarette in your mouth, meditation provides you that extra space of calm and rationality before you light it up. You will then be able to take action.

 

Before responding, take a moment to reflect

 

Meditation practice improves your capacity to quickly recognize when your mind wanders. Slowing down your brain's rapid churning helps you to better understand your thoughts and sensations.

 

This capacity will present itself in your daily life after a few months of practice as more space between an emotional trigger and your reaction. This frees you from your conditioned responses, allowing you to engage with present challenges more wisely and efficiently.

 

So, if your automatic reaction to stress is to pick up a cigarette, meditation gives you that extra space of calm and logic before you do. You will then be allowed to proceed.
