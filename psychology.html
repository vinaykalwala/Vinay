Abnormal Psychology

The area of psychology known as "abnormal psychology" is concerned with examining odd patterns of thinking, emotion, and behaviour that may or may not be indicative of a mental condition. The majority of the time, this area of psychology deals with behaviour in a clinical setting, even if many behaviors might be seen as deviant. There has been a long history of efforts to comprehend and rein in behaviour that has been regarded abnormal or deviant (statistically, functionally, ethically, or in other ways), and the methods used have frequently varied among cultures.

Clinical psychologists are at the forefront of the treatment of mental health issues through the use of psychological tests, interviews, observations of behaviour, and various psychological treatments

Cognitive psychology

Cognitive psychology is the scientific study of human cognition, which includes all of our mental capacities such as perception, learning, memory, thinking, reasoning, and comprehending. The term "cognition" comes from the Latin word cognoscere, which means "to know." Cognitive psychology is the study of how humans learn and utilize knowledge or information. It is strongly connected to cognitive science, which is very multidisciplinary, and is influenced by artificial intelligence, computer science, philosophy, anthropology, linguistics, biology, physics, and neuroscience.

Developmental psychology

Developmental psychology, commonly referred to as Human Development, is the academic study of the gradual psychological alterations that take place in growing adults. It used to focus on young children and newborns, but has now expanded to include all stages of life, including puberty and aging. This discipline studies change in a variety of areas, including as problem-solving skills, conceptual understanding, language learning, moral understanding, and identity development, in addition to motor skills and other psycho-physiological processes.
